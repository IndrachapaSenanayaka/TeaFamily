Literature Survey

Sri lanka is the 3rd highest valued tea exporter in the world[4]

Figure 1. Annual tea production 2006 - 2017 [5]

In Sri Lanka, the tea sector plays a significant role in agriculture, with approximately 16% of the country's arable land dedicated to tea cultivation. Interestingly, tea smallholders are the backbone of this industry, as they operate on 60% of the total tea land and contribute to over 70% of the total tea production. According to the Tea Control Act, any tea land ranging from 20 perches to 10 acres is classified as 'Tea Small Holdings,' and there are nearly 500,000 such smallholders in the country.

As per the annual report of the Small Tea Estate Development Authority (TSHDA) in 2021, the Tea Inspection Officer to Tea Smallholder ratio stands at 1:3374, underscoring the need for more effective monitoring and support systems. Recognizing this, a surveillance and predictive information system has been identified as a valuable resource to enhance farm decision-making and ensure the sustainable growth of the tea sector.

Research Problem & Solution

Research Problem

How to classify the foliar diseases in the tea plantation, and how to predict the tea yield, cost, tea demand and tea price, and provide surveillance to people in real-time?

Tea smallholders often lack the financial resources and knowledge to manage their crops effectively, leading to potential disease issues that can reduce crop yield and impact their livelihoods. Proper cost estimation and input management are crucial for tea farmers to prevent financial losses and maintain profitability. Additionally, factors like weather conditions, soil quality, and cultivation practices affect tea yield, but small plantation owners may lack knowledge in these areas. Furthermore, there is a lack of consultants to assist tea smallholders in predicting income, selecting suitable tea varieties, and forecasting future prices and yields, making it challenging for them to plan and improve their cultivation practices.


Problem statement and user feedback

Proposed Solution

We aim to address various challenges through the development of a mobile-responsive web-based application. This application will serve as a comprehensive solution, offering features such as disease diagnosis and control for tea plants. Users, particularly smallholder tea farmers, can upload photos of affected tea leaves, and the system will identify the disease type and provide corresponding control recommendations. Additionally, we intend to create a cost estimation platform for tea cultivation. By inputting factors like plantation size, labor, fertilizer, and pest control costs, the platform will provide accurate cost estimates, revealing valuable insights from the data. Furthermore, we plan to implement a system for predicting tea crop yields, utilizing factors like tea variety, soil type, and cultivation practices. This will empower farmers to make informed decisions to optimize yield and sustainability. Lastly, we employ the ARIMA algorithm to analyze tea market dynamics, including demand and pricing trends, enabling growers to anticipate market shifts and enhance the efficiency and profitability of tea production in Sri Lanka.

Research Objectives

Foliar disease diagnosis and control in tea plants and predict the disease using environmental data

The first objective is to identify tea leaves effectively, with a particular focus on diagnosing foliar diseases within the tea plant. Our approach also involves determining the stage of infection in order to provide tailored recommendations for mitigating the damage based on the severity of the disease. Additionally, we aim to predict the occurrence of blister blight disease by analyzing environmental data, and subsequently, we will develop a comprehensive prevention plan to proactively address the predicted disease. This multifaceted approach will assist tea growers in managing their crops more efficiently and ensuring a healthier tea plant population.

Predict the cost of the tea cultivation

The second objective of this study is to create an application designed for estimating the expenses associated with tea cultivation. This application will leverage an Arima Model to provide accurate estimations of the costs involved in tea cultivation. The utilization of this model will enable tea growers to make informed decisions about their cultivation expenses, ultimately aiding them in managing their resources efficiently and maximizing their yields.

Predict tea yield considering the Sri Lankan tea plant varieties

The third objective is to employ various factors, including plant branches, soil type, and cultivation practices, to estimate the potential yield of a tea crop. This estimation will serve as a valuable tool for tea farmers, enabling them to make informed decisions and optimize their production methods to maximize both yield and profitability. By harnessing these factors and leveraging data-driven insights, tea farmers can implement strategies that lead to improved crop yields and more efficient cultivation practices.

Forecast the tea demand and market price in the Sri Lankan tea industry

The fourth objective is to predict the demand for tea and market prices within the Sri Lankan tea industry. This forecasting endeavor aims to provide valuable insights to tea producers, traders, and stakeholders. By leveraging data analysis and predictive modeling techniques, we intend to offer forecasts that can assist in making informed decisions regarding tea production, pricing, and distribution. These insights will play a pivotal role in optimizing the tea industry's operations, ensuring a more efficient allocation of resources, and enhancing overall productivity.

Methodology

Figure 2. High Level Architecture of the system.

Surveillance and Predictive Information System for Tea Smallholdings consists of 4 main components. They are;

  1. Foliar disease diagnosis and control in tea plants and predict the disease using environmental data
  2. Predict the cost of the tea cultivation
  3. Predict tea yield considering the Sri Lankan tea plant varieties
  4. Forecast the tea demand and market price in the Sri Lankan tea industry

Fig 2 illustrates the overall system diagram, comprising four main components. In component A, we focus on foliar disease diagnosis and control in tea plants, specifically predicting Blister blight disease using environmental data. This involves using an image dataset for training a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model to categorize healthy and infected tea leaves. Additionally, a separate CNN is educated to identify disease stages visually. Real-time environmental data, including temperature, humidity, and rainfall, is gathered to train a Support Vector Machine (SVM) for forecasting Blister blight occurrences, enabling proactive prevention.

In component B, we predict the cost of tea cultivation by analyzing historical data on labor, weedicide, fertilizer expenses, and land size. After preprocessing, we employ an Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model to anticipate cultivation costs based on user inputs like location and land size.

Component C deals with predicting tea yield considering various tea plant varieties. We use a dataset encompassing factors such as tea type, land size, previous yields, weather, and fertilizer use. Linear regression techniques are applied after data preparation to enhance yield prediction based on user inputs like location and fertilizer amount.

Lastly, in component D, we forecast tea demand and market prices in the Sri Lankan tea industry. Historical market pricing and demand data undergo preprocessing before Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models, designed for time-series analysis, are employed for forecasting. Users can select a year for demand and price predictions, and accuracy metrics like mean absolute error and mean squared error are used to evaluate forecast precision. These components collectively provide a comprehensive system for managing tea plantations, from disease control and cost estimation to yield optimization and market forecasting.
